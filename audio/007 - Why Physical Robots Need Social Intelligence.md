Cooperative AI: Machines Must Learn to Find Common Ground (May 2021) + Embodied Large Language Models Enable Robots to Complete Complex Tasks in Unpredictable Environments (April 2025)

In this episode we pair a 2021 Nature Comment with a 2025 Nature Machine Intelligence article to ask why physical robots still struggle to be genuinely social. Allan Dafoe, Yoram Bachrach, Gillian Hadfield, Eric Horvitz, Kate Larson, and Thore Graepel — writing from Oxford, DeepMind, Toronto, OpenAI, Microsoft, and Waterloo — argue that AI has been steeped in methodological individualism: a solitary agent confronting a non-social environment, or at best two-player zero-sum games like chess and Go. They lay out four elements of cooperative intelligence (understanding, communication, commitment, and norms and institutions), three research clusters (AI–AI, AI–human, and AI for human–human cooperation), and hold up Axelrod's Tit for Tat and mixed-motive games like Hanabi, robot soccer, and Diplomacy as the testbeds we should be training on.

We then turn to Ruaridh Mon-Williams and colleagues at Edinburgh, MIT, Princeton, and the Alan Turing Institute, whose ELLMER framework (Embodied LLM-enabled Robot) pairs GPT-4 with retrieval-augmented generation over a curated knowledge base of motion primitives to drive a Kinova Gen3 seven-degree-of-freedom arm through long-horizon tasks — making coffee for a tired human, drawing DALL-E-generated random animals on a plate, handing over a pen — while integrating force feedback from an ATI multi-axis sensor and vision from an Azure Kinect with Grounded-Segment-Anything. We walk through the key results (RAG lifts the GPT-4 faithfulness score from 0.74 to 0.88 against the VoxPoser baseline, force feedback enables pouring accurate to roughly minus 5.4 grams per 100 grams, zero-shot object detection via Grounded DINO averaging 52.5 on COCO), and close on why ELLMER, impressive as it is, still only instantiates a sliver of Dafoe and colleagues' agenda: it cooperates with one user, in a known kitchen, without bargaining, trust, credible commitments, or institutional norms — and what a genuinely cooperative physical robot would have to look like.

Reference: Dafoe, A., Bachrach, Y., Hadfield, G., Horvitz, E., Larson, K., & Graepel, T. (2021). Cooperative AI: machines must learn to find common ground. Nature, 593(7857), 33–36. https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-021-01170-0

Reference: Mon-Williams, R., Li, G., Long, R., Du, W., & Lucas, C. G. (2025). Embodied large language models enable robots to complete complex tasks in unpredictable environments. Nature Machine Intelligence, 7(4), 592–601. https://doi.org/10.1038/s42256-025-01005-x
