Embodied Conversational Agents: Representation and Intelligence in User Interfaces (Winter 2001)

In this episode we unpack an article by Justine Cassell, published in AAAI's AI Magazine, that pushes back against the "disappearing computer" dream of ubiquitous, invisible ambient computing. Her counter-claim: users cannot easily interact with what they cannot see, and the best example of located, legible intelligence humans have is the body. To build interfaces people can actually hold a conversation with, we need Embodied Conversational Agents — systems that carry speech, hand gesture, eye gaze, head movement, and facial expression together, because that is how humans do face-to-face conversation.

We walk through her brief history of embodied interfaces, from the eighteenth-century automata of Droz's Writing Boy and Von Kempelen's Speaking Machine to present-day avatars, and then through her FMBT model (Functions, Modalities, Behaviors, Time, pronounced "fembot") — a multiplicity-of-representations framework that separates propositional content from interactional regulation, treats conversation as modality-free, and insists a hand gesture can be just as likely as a word to carry meaning. We then cover her implemented agent Rea, a virtual real estate agent built on the FMBT model, walking through its architecture (Input Manager, Understanding and Decision modules split into Interactional and Propositional Processing, Generation, Action Scheduler, and a hardwired-reactions path for sub-200-millisecond responses), the SPUD natural-language generation engine, and the KQML inter-module messaging, and close on why — a full generation before today's LLM-powered avatars — she was already arguing against Rodney Brooks-style "intelligence without representation" for any system that has to actually converse with a human.

Reference: Cassell, J. (2001). Embodied Conversational Agents: Representation and Intelligence in User Interfaces. AI Magazine, 22(4).
