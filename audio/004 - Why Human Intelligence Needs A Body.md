The Development of Embodied Cognition: Six Lessons from Babies (2005)

In this episode we unpack a paper by Linda Smith (Psychology) and Michael Gasser (Computer Science) at Indiana University, published in MIT Press's Artificial Life. The argument: if you want to build intelligence, do not start by coding up symbols and inference — start by building something that can grow up, because intelligence emerges from an agent's sensorimotor interaction with a physical, social, and linguistic world, and the flexible, inventive quality that characterizes human minds is inseparable from the particular developmental path babies take.

We walk through the six lessons the authors draw from developmental psychology for building embodied agents: Be Multimodal (Edelman's reentry mappings, the transparent-container studies from Diamond and from Titzer, Thelen, and Smith, and the Tucker-Ellis motor-compatibility task), Be Incremental (why infants' prematurity and developmental ordering matter, the A-not-B error and its tie to self-locomotion, and why neural networks sometimes fail on full datasets but succeed with staged input), Be Physical (passive walkers and coupled-pendulum models of bipedal locomotion, Ballard and colleagues' offloading of short-term memory into the world), Explore (spontaneous non-goal-directed movement, the week-by-week development of reaching, and the infant conjugate-reinforcement paradigm), Be Social (mother-infant face-to-face coupling, imitation cycles, and multimodal scaffolding of early words), and Learn a Language (symbols as shared communicative systems and DeLoache's scale-model task showing why too much iconicity can actually break symbol use). We close on what this means for AI research that wants to reach the open-ended flexibility of human intelligence.

Reference: Smith, L., & Gasser, M. (2005). The Development of Embodied Cognition: Six Lessons from Babies. Artificial Life, 11(1–2), 13–29. https://doi.org/10.1162/1064546053278973
